Arts

ISKCON (International Society for Krishna Consciousness) integrates a rich array of arts to express devotion, drawing from ancient Indian and Vaishnava traditions. Here’s a brief overview of the key artistic areas:

1.VANDE (Vaisnava Arts for a New Devotional Era)

ISKCON has created VANDE, a community to nurture artistic talent. The initiative aims to engage the public through high-quality presentations in various art forms, including fine arts, theatre, dance, music, and architecture.

2. Dance & Music

-Music: Devotees like Ilan Chester (Havi das), a Latin Grammy winner, use music to spread Krishna's teachings. ISKCON temples host musical performances and classes focused on classical Indian forms.

- Dance: Traditional dances like Bharata Natyam, Kathak, and Odissi are performed to express devotion. Bhakti Kalalayam Academy in Florida trains students in these art forms.

3.Fine Arts

ISKCON artists create paintings depicting Lord Krishna and His divine realm, blending Eastern and Western styles. These artworks illustrate sacred texts and decorate ISKCON temples worldwide.

4. Drama

Theatre is used to narrate stories from scriptures like the Ramayana and Krishna Lila.Bhaktimarga Swamileads dramatic performances at festivals such as the Durban Rathayatra and Buenos Aires Rathayatra.

5. Architecture

Iskcon temples showcase traditional and neo-Vedic architecture. Notable examples include the Spanish Fork temple in Utah, modeled after a 15th-century palace, and the gold-domed temple in West Virginia, known as America’s Taj Mahal.

6. Literature

The Bhaktivedanta Book Trust (BBT) has published over 400 million books, offering translations of sacred texts, commentaries, and illustrations, making Vaishnava philosophy accessible worldwide.

ISKCON’s artistic endeavors help spread devotion and philosophy through diverse, vibrant cultural expressions.




Music-The kirtan

The power of sound is central to the spiritual practices of the Vedas, which emphasize the use of sound to awaken our original, blissful spiritual consciousness. In the current age, the medium of sound is seen as especially effective for spiritual transformation. The joyous experience of chanting and singing the Divine's glories—through a practice calledsonic theology—awakens both the performer and the audience to a deeper spiritual understanding of the self and the Supreme Self (God).

Devotional Music in Bhakti

Devotional music, or bhakti music, has developed into various styles and traditions. A popular saying by the Madras Music Academy of South India, “kanu bina gita nahi” (Without Krishna, there is no song), encapsulates this connection between music and divine consciousness.

Bhakti Music

Kirtan: The Heart of Devotional Sound

Kirtan, a call-and-response chanting, was popularized by Sri Chaitanya Mahaprabhus over 500 years ago in India and is now a global practice. It involves the repetition of mantras and hymns, often accompanied by traditional instruments such as the mrdanga drum, kartals (cymbals), and the harmonium. Today, kirtan blends traditional instruments with modern ones like saxophones, bass guitars, and various percussion instruments. The chanting starts with a slow, melodic rhythm and gradually crescendos into exuberant singing and dancing.

Kirtan in ISKCON

Kirtan is a central part of ISKCON practices, performed daily during worship services called aratis. Devotees also engage in kirtan at home or in public spaces, such as parks and city streets, spreading the spiritual vibration to everyone around them. The sound vibration of kirtan helps purify the heart and removes negative emotions like greed, envy, and anger, contributing to peace and spiritual progress.

Whether in its traditional form or modernized style, bhakti music's primary purpose is to foster spiritual awakening and lead listeners into deep states of meditation and bliss.




Festivals

Festivals are an integral part of ISKCON. With myriad of colourful decorations, multi-course feasts, elaborate rituals, kirtans, dance and dramas, the festivals makes spiritual progress a fun-filled experience. The original Vedic flavor is thus revived, and anyone who participates in them can vouch that there is no modern day substitute for these soul-satisfying experiences.



1.Janmastami

Sri Krishna JanmastamiJanmastami – the birth of Lord Krishna – is one of the biggest events of the year. It is the Vaishnava equivalent of Christmas in that devotees celebrate God’s advent 5,000 years ago into this material world from the spiritual. The name Krishna means “All-Attractive.” Lord Krishna exhibits six opulences in full: beauty, knowledge, fame, wealth, strength, and humility. Thus, He is worshipped as “The Supreme All-Attractive Personality of Godhead.”

Today, Janmastami is celebrated across the globe with unparalleled grandeur at all ISKCON centers. All day performances of music, drama, dance and chanting in glorification of the enchanting pastimes of the Lord, lead up the midnight hour at which Lord Krishna appeared. A midnight service accompanied by kirtan and dancing is followed by a multi-course vegetarian feast for all.




2. Rath Yatra-Festival of Chariots

With the exception of Kumbha Mela, the Rath Yatra traditionally held in Puri, India attracts the largest gathering of pilgrims in India. During this festival, the beautifully bedecked Deities of Jagannath (Lord of the universe) , Baladev (Krishna’s brother) and Subhadra (Krishna’s sister) are taken out on three giant chariots specially constructed for the parade.

According to scriptures, even though Krishna became a great King of Dwarka, his childhood was spent amongst simple cowherd boys and girls in the village of Vrindavana. This mood of wanting to bring back Krishna to the playful pastimes of Vrindavana signifies the inner meaning of Rath Yatra. When devotees pull the ropes of the cart, it symbolizes the pulling of the Lord into our hearts.

In the late 1960s, A.C. Bhaktivedanta Swami Prabhupada inaugurated this joyous event in North America. With roots steeped in spiritual traditions, the festival is now celebrated in every major city around the world, including London, Paris, Durban, Sydney, Mumbai and New York. For instance , in Los Angeles, more than 50,000 people take part in the colorful walk, pulling three giant 40-foot tall chariots. Exhibitions and stage performances often accompany the parade, amidst melodious singing, chanting, drumming,dancing and feasting.



3. Diwali

Diwali, or the Festival of Lights, marks the Hindu New Year, and is associated with many of the Supreme Lord’s pastimes on earth. Diwali is the best known of Vedic holidays and is today marked by events in places as varied as ISKCON temples and America’s White House.

For ISKCON members, this day is most notable because it marks the return of Lord Rama, an incarnation of Krishna, to his kingdom of Ayodhya, after his defeat of the evil king Ravana. Rama was welcomed by all the citizens who lit lamps or dipas illuminating the sky for his joyous return. It also marks a day during Lord Krishna’s advent when He dispatched the demoniac Narakasura.

Diwali thus signifies the victory of light over darkness and good over evil. The festival also symbolizes a time for spiritual renewal by lighting a lamp within our own hearts for spiritual enlightenment and by welcoming the Lord into our lives. The festival is often celebrated with elaborate firework displays, lighting Diwali lamps and feasting.



4.Holi-Festival of colours

Traditionally, Holi announces the arrival of spring. It is also connected with a special pastime of Lord Krishna wherein He and his devotees playfully sprayed each other with colored water. Reenacted each year with zest, Holi is today an event that draws tens of thousands to ISKCON temples for a day of music, dance, feasting, and exuberant throwing of colors, or powdered dyes, upon all who are bold enough, or fun loving enough, to participate.
